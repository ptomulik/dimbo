Virtually each engineering discipline is equipped with a computer software supporting design and analysis in that field. In the scope of multibody system dynamics one can count at least seven commercial packages suitable for the simulation and analysis of mechanisms. Each one provides its own procedures for analysis and optimization as well as database formats used to store models.

In multidisciplinary projects, or projects conducted by collaborating groups, it sometimes happens that two or more independently prepared models have to be analysed jointly as a single system. As long as models are compatible with some common simulation package, they may be merged into single model and loaded by that tool for analysis. Things become more difficult when models are incompatible. For related disciplines, such as the multibody system dynamics with finite element analysis or control systems, one can utilize predefined co-simulation interfaces provided by the engineering packages. Such interfaces usually support single counterpart software. Extending simulation environment to allow free interaction between N incompatible tools requires Nx(N-1) interfaces to be developed

DIMBO follows some design principles of MpCCI where the computational environment may be split into two layers such that the lower layer contains simulators with their adapters, whereas the higher layer provides a service responsible for synchronization/coupling.

Computational codes interfaced by DIMBO are assumed to represent multibody subsystems. First supported code is our in-house routine for rigid multibody systems based on OpenCL. In general, two kinds of computational codes are assumed to be supported at some point:

  • routines which evaluate equations of motion at prescribed time instants,
  • simulators which integrate by their own the underlying differential equations.

The resultant computational algorithm is a kind of distributed integrator of Differential Algebraic Equations. It's iterative and it's based on methods known as dynamic iteration or waveform relaxation. Especially tailored to subsystem coupled by constraints is the waveform-Newton algorithm.
